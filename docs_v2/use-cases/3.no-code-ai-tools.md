# No-code AI tools for business teams

The people who best understand a workflow are rarely the people who can write a service to automate it. This page explains why letting business teams build their own AI tools is valuable, how DIAL Quick Apps make it possible without code, and where the no-code boundary sits. It is for decision-makers judging the approach and for the builders or champions who would enable it.

## Why this matters

When every AI tool requires an engineering ticket, two things happen: the backlog grows, and the people closest to the problem stop asking. The result is a handful of polished applications and a long tail of unmet needs. Domain experts — analysts, support leads, operations staff — could express most of those needs themselves if the platform let them, but a raw model API is not something a non-developer can safely assemble into a tool.

The value of no-code AI building is throughput and ownership. A subject-matter expert composes a working tool in an afternoon, iterates on it directly, and owns it, while the platform team keeps the governance that matters.

## What you can do with DIAL

[Quick Apps](../building-with-dial/apps/quick-apps/0.index.md) let someone build an AI application by configuration rather than code: pick a language model, write system instructions, and attach tools. The result is a shareable application that behaves like any other on the platform.

The tools come from [Tool Sets](../building-with-dial/apps/quick-apps/quick-app-2/tool-sets/0.index.md) — reusable connectors to web APIs, MCP servers, and other DIAL applications. A Quick App can call a weather API, query an internal service, or hand off to a Custom App, and it can loop across several tools and agents to complete a multi-step task. Because Quick Apps run on the platform, they inherit access control, sharing, and cost limits without their authors configuring any of it.

Common shapes include retrieval-style assistants over a fixed set of sources, tools that call external APIs through Tool Sets, and multi-step workflows that combine several agents.

## How you'd build it

- **Learn the model.** [What are Quick Apps](../building-with-dial/apps/quick-apps/0.index.md) explains the configuration-driven approach and what a Quick App can do.
- **Connect tools.** [Tool Sets](../building-with-dial/apps/quick-apps/quick-app-2/tool-sets/0.index.md) covers defining and registering the connectors a Quick App calls.
- **Orchestrate.** [Working with tools and agents](../building-with-dial/apps/quick-apps/quick-app-2/working-with-tools-and-agents.md) shows how to compose multi-step behavior.
- **Share safely.** [Sharing and permissions](../building-with-dial/apps/quick-apps/quick-app-2/tool-sets/3.sharing-and-permissions.md) governs who can use a tool set and its credentials.

## Why DIAL over the alternatives

Standalone no-code AI builders such as Dify or Flowise are quick to start with but live outside your governance: separate identity, separate cost tracking, separate place for data to sit. DIAL Quick Apps trade some of that standalone polish for being native to the platform, so a tool a business user builds is governed, shareable, and cost-controlled the same way a developer's application is. The [DIAL vs app builders](../understand-dial/positioning/comparisons/2.dial-vs-app-builders.md) comparison covers this in detail.

The no-code boundary is real. When a tool needs custom dependencies, external network access you control, or its own databases, it has outgrown a Quick App and wants a Custom App. [When to use which app type](../building-with-dial/apps/when-to-use-which.md) draws that line, and a common pattern is to prototype as a Quick App, then move performance-critical logic into a Custom App while keeping the Quick App as the orchestration layer.
